What exactly are chamber tombs?

Chamber tombs are made up of a central shaft with niches carved into the sides for multiple depositions, often buried at different times. This is a radical change from before, when the standard was one body per grave.

This type of burial emerged around the middle of the 7th C BC. The majority of chamber tombs contained adults, male or female. Our tomb has three adults, presumed to be male (more on that later). One question we wanted to explore in our research is why this tomb was so different from other burials in the area; as you can see from the map, located temporally and physically nearby is a sarcophagus tomb 25.

Interestingly, different types of burial were going on during this time, and chamber tombs did not “replace” one-person inhumations or even cremation. While we are going to suggest broader implications of the rise of the chamber tomb, it is important to keep in mind that these were not the only option for burial—although, as we will show, it was certainly a popular choice for elites.

Trends and Their Social Implications

The new burial practice of using chamber tombs can be used to analyze political, religious, and social changes occurring during the Archaic period. Sarah Willemsen studied Crustumerium, but she writes that because chamber tombs have been found throughout Central Italy, we can study these tombs to understand overarching trends throughout the surrounding area, including Gabii.

An interview with Troy

A closer look at elite grave goods

Troy Samuels is a Ph.D candidate at the University of Michigan and has worked on the Gabii project for seven years. His research focuses on elite status and textile production at Gabii, very relevant topics for the grave we studied. Our interview with Troy highlights the possible purpose and context behind the type and size of the tomb, the goods found in the tomb, and the status of the people buried. As if the interview couldn’t have gone any better, we found out Troy actually discovered the tomb by falling in it!
